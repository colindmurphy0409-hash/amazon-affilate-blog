You do not need a $200 countertop blender to make creamy soup, whip up a smoothie, or emulsify salad dressing. A good immersion blender — also called a stick or hand blender — handles those jobs in the pot or cup you already have, with less cleanup and less counter space.

We compared the best immersion blenders under $50 on Amazon for power, attachments, and real-world usability. All four picks sit at or below the $50 mark at the time of writing; prices change, so check Amazon for current numbers.

## What to look for in a budget hand blender

**Motor power** matters for thick soups and frozen fruit, but wattage alone is misleading. Blade design and shaft shape often matter more for smooth results.

**Speed control** ranges from a simple two-speed switch to 12-step dials. More speeds help when you are trying not to splatter hot soup across the counter.

**Attachments** expand what one motor can do. A whisk handles cream and eggs; a chopper bowl minces garlic and herbs; a frother foams milk for coffee.

**Shaft material** should be stainless steel if you blend hot liquids often. Plastic shafts can stain and are harder to keep odor-free over time.

## 1. Mueller Ultra-Stick — Best overall under $50

The Mueller Ultra-Stick is the default recommendation in this price range for good reason. The 500W motor, nine speeds, and turbo button handle everyday blending without the premium price tag. You get a stainless shaft, whisk, milk frother, and measuring beaker — more accessories than most competitors include at twice the price.

It is not perfect. The motor housing is plastic, and heavy daily users occasionally report burnout after a year or two. For occasional soups, sauces, and smoothies, it delivers more capability per dollar than anything else here.

**Best for:** Most home cooks who want one affordable stick blender with useful extras.

## 2. Braun MultiQuick 5 MQ505 — Best blend quality at $50

Braun’s PowerBell blade and SplashControl shaft are the real story. The MQ505 produces noticeably smoother purees with less suction and splatter than typical budget sticks. You trade accessory variety for engineering: the base kit includes a whisk and beaker, but no chopper bowl.

Speed control is simpler — two settings plus turbo — so it is less flexible than the Mueller or KOIOS. If your main job is silky soup and you can spend closer to $50, the Braun is worth the step up.

**Best for:** Cooks who care about texture and build quality more than accessory count.

## 3. KOIOS 5-in-1 — Best accessory kit for small kitchens

The KOIOS set replaces a stick blender, mini chopper, whisk, and frother in one drawer-friendly package. Twelve speeds plus turbo give fine control, and the included chopper bowl handles small batches of salsa, nuts, or baby food without a separate appliance.

Peak wattage on the label oversells sustained power — expect performance in line with other budget sticks, not a commercial unit. Still, for apartment kitchens that need maximum function from minimum space, the kit value is hard to beat.

**Best for:** Small kitchens, dorms, and anyone who wants a chopper without buying a second device.

## 4. Cuisinart CSB-179 Smart Stick — Best brand-name kit

Cuisinart brings a variable speed slide control, 3-cup chopper/grinder, whisk, and measuring cup, plus a 3-year warranty. The 300W motor is the lowest here on paper, but the blade and shaft design handle typical soup-and-sauce work reliably.

Some owners note that attachment plastic feels less solid than the stainless blending shaft. The slide control takes a light touch at high speed to avoid splashing. If brand trust and warranty length matter to you, the CSB-179 is a safe pick.

**Best for:** Buyers who want a recognizable kitchen brand with chopper and whisk included.

## FAQ

### Is 300 watts enough for an immersion blender?

For soup, sauce, and soft smoothies, yes. Wattage helps with thick or frozen ingredients, but blade design matters too — the Braun MQ505 often out-blends higher-wattage sticks on purees.

### Do I need a chopper attachment?

Not if you already own a food processor or mini chopper. If counter space is tight, the KOIOS or Cuisinart kits save buying a second appliance.

### Can immersion blenders crush ice?

Budget models struggle with full ice cubes. Use crushed ice or blend liquids first, then add ice briefly on turbo. None of these picks replace a dedicated blender for frozen drinks.

### Immersion blender vs countertop blender?

Stick blenders win on cleanup and storage. Countertop blenders win on frozen drinks and large batches. Many kitchens keep both; if you pick one under $50, start with a stick blender for soups and sauces.
